# Prior Art

Existing games and tools that overlap with the MMODAW design, and what to learn from each.

---

## IV-V-I: The Harmony Card Game

A physical card game where players compete to build the best harmonic progressions using good voice leading and cadences. The closest philosophical predecessor to Scale Navigator found so far: functional harmony (T/S/D) turned into an accessible, rules-driven system where non-theorists can succeed.

- **Format:** 2–4 players, 30–60 min, ages 12+, $10 (currently Sold Out)
- **Source:** https://musicteachertools.myshopify.com/products/iv-v-i

### Who made it

The solo creation of **Dr. Rafael Hernandez**, a composer and academic — now Deputy Provost (Academic Resources & Planning) and Professor of Music at Cal State East Bay, previously chair of the Music Department and director of its Multimedia Graduate Program (D.M. Indiana, M.M. UT Austin, B.M. Virginia Commonwealth). Not a game-industry designer but a self-described "composer, teacher, and nerd" working at the intersection of "music, technology, and gaming." He describes himself as having created "functional music typefaces" and "a functional harmony card game (yes, such a thing does exist)," and brings "artists, musicians, programmers, and other like minds together to make fantastic interactive artworks."

It appears to be a one-person effort — no illustrator, co-designer, or publisher credited — self-published through his own micro-company **MusicTeacherTools** (California, since 2007), alongside his MusicEd font family. His YouTube handle is **cheetomoskeeto** (the account that posted the 2009 tutorials and commented as the creator in 2020).

### Why and how it was developed

Hernandez explained the origin in a 2020 r/musictheory thread:

> "The game came about as a result of my teaching collegiate music theory. I found my students could benefit from a play-based approach to content that was highly logical and rules driven."

His core design goal was accessibility:

> "It's designed so that anyone can play the game, even if they know nothing about music (those folks often win 😆)."

That win-condition twist — novices sometimes beating trained musicians — is a deliberate feature: the game encodes harmonic grammar into card-adjacency rules ("play windows") rather than requiring theory knowledge. The seven-part instructional video series was posted July 2009, placing the public rollout around 2007–2009, within the same classroom-tool impulse as the MusicEd fonts.

### Mechanics

- Each player is dealt 7 cards. On a turn, draw from the draw or discard pile, then build a phrase of up to three harmony cards, played to the left or right of your existing phrase.
- Harmony cards are labeled by function — **T** (tonic), **S** (subdominant / pre-dominant), **D** (dominant) — and each has a **"play window"** dictating what can precede and follow it (a "5" can be preceded by a T or S and followed by a T). This encodes real voice-leading grammar as a constraint system.
- **Special Harmony cards** (rainbow) like "sequence by fifths" and "chordal expansion" add flexibility.
- **Style cards** (named after composers) change scoring rules — e.g. the Shostakovich card inverts the usual penalty logic, rewarding part-writing errors.

### The response

Enthusiastic but decidedly niche — a cult favorite among music educators and theory nerds, not a mass-market hit.

- **2012 r/musictheory:** users called it "incredible," "fantastic," "absolutely amazing," several buying a set immediately; teachers said they'd use it in class. Main critique: disappointment that Wagner, Schoenberg, and Stravinsky weren't included as composer/style cards.
- **2020 r/musictheory:** when a user proposed building "a game to teach functional harmony," the top reaction — "This is the nerdiest shit I've ever seen. Please, keep up the good work" (144 upvotes) — captured the vibe; dozens replied "I'd buy that game." Hernandez stepped in to note his game already existed.
- **Substantive critiques (design-oriented):** one user argued it "would work better as software that can also play the chords," since hearing harmony matters for learning; another (a game designer) warned that theory games often "focus too much on the theory and don't try to make a fun game" and stressed the core mechanics must stand on their own. Many compared it to Uno and floated Uno-style twists (deceptive cadences reversing turn order, pivot-chord effects).

No BoardGameGeek listing and no formal press reviews — footprint is almost entirely word-of-mouth in music-education circles.

### What to take from it

- **The critiques of IV-V-I are exactly the problems this platform solves.** The single biggest complaint — "it should play the chords; learning needs sound" — is a limitation of a physical card game that an interactive/audio system inherently answers. Same with the "make it modulate / justify a weird chord" and "software version" wishes.
- The **"play window"** is exactly our harmonic-transition constraint — the same idea Ensemble Jammer uses to keep every instrument inside the shared harmonic state. Proof that a constraint system on transitions reads as a *game* to non-theorists.
- **Symbol-based, training-free access** — and novices winning — validates the "no music theory required" premise.
- **Composer Style cards → NPC composers.** IV-V-I already names cards after composers that change the rules of play. That's our NPCs-as-composers mechanic in card form: characters who embody a harmonic language and change how you craft.
- **Rafael Hernandez may be worth a direct conversation** — the closest predecessor to this work, an academic who explicitly bridges musicians and programmers to make interactive experiences.
